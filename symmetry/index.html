Chapter 1: Symmetry

The year is 1800. And we’ve gotten quite good at solving equations, but in the quest to find the solution to polynomials of degree 4, 5, 6 and higher, we needed a bit of help. This, is where an 18-year-old named Évariste Galois plays a role in this. He used something he called a “group”, laying down the foundations of the theory. Promptly, Gauss had done a blistering amount of contributions to modular arithmetic, which led to him discovering a bit about groups and subgroups. Soon, academia saw the usefulness of the group, and how many applications it had, from geometry to cryptography, from music theory to chemistry, quickly realizing that group theory is here to stay.

Introduction

The universe is an enormous direct product of representations of symmetry groups. – Hermann Weyl

When we think about symmetry in geometry class, we’d often remind ourselves that circles, triangles and rectangles fall into that category. You could reflect them and rotate them, and they’d look identical.

As you've seen, circles have an infinite amount of symmetries, you can rotate it a thousand degrees and they'd still remain symmetrical. In fact, rotating it a thousand degrees would be identical to a 280-degree rotation. The ways you can rotate it just go on forever. Listing out each and every one of them like with our square would be impossible.

However, one important aspect of group theory is seeing how symmetries play nicely with each other. For example, if you were to turn a square and then flip it diagonally, it’s the exact same as a vertical flip. We could say that it equals a turn and flip, after all.

90° + Diagonal Flip = Horizontal Flip

But considering all of these interactions between symmetries, understanding how each and every one relates to another would be very, very difficult. This, is where diagrams play a huge role.

This, is what's called a Cayley diagram, they're like a map, guiding us from A to B except with symmetries. Say for example you wished to achieve the bottom right symmetry. All you'd have to do is just follow the diagram. This particular diagram however, only shows the interactions between flipping a square, with red representing a vertical flip, and blue representing a horizontal flip. If rotating was involved, it'd get quite huge.
